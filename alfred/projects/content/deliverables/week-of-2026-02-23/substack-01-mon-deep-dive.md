## Topic: Why Learning Skills Beats Learning Specific Platforms

Most people start building with AI on a specific platform Lovable, Replit, or Bolt.

The problem is these tools hide what’s actually happening behind a fancy interface.

So if your entire ability to build is tied to one platform, you’re at the mercy of their decisions.

Prices go up, they go out of business, etc…

There’s a better approach.

Instead of mastering a specific tool, learn the process that works across all of them.

There are really only 4 skills that matter when it comes to building with AI:

1. Describing what you want clearly (prompt engineering)

This is the big one. Whether you’re using Cursor, Claude Code, or whatever comes out next month, you need to be able to clearly articulate what you’re building. This goes beyond writing good prompts, it’s learning how to think clearly about your product. What does it do? Who is it for? What should happen when someone clicks this button?

If you can describe what you want well, every AI tool gets better.

2. Giving proper context (context engineering)

AI coding tools only know what you tell them. Context engineering is learning how to curate what the AI sees, giving it the right information so it actually produces useful output. This means things like writing rules files, setting up project docs, feeding it the right parts of your codebase, and knowing what to include and what to leave out. The better you get at this, the better every AI tool works for you. And this skill transfers to every single one of them.

3. Testing and debugging

Every tool produces output you need to check. Does it do what I asked? Did it break something else? Learning to test your app systematically is a skill that works everywhere. This is one of those things that separates people who build demos from people who build real stuff.

4. Deploying and maintaining

Getting your app live and keeping it running. This is infrastructure knowledge, domains, hosting, databases, environment variables.

The AI tool landscape is going to look completely different 12 months from now.

But these skills will still be applicable.

My advice is focus on the workflow, not the UI.

When something breaks, learn why it broke, not just how to click the right button to fix it in that one tool.

That’s how you make sure you’re actually learning something that lasts.
