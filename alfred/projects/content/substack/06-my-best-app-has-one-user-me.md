Subject: My best app has one user. Me.

I want to talk about something that doesn't get enough attention in the "build an app" conversation.

Not everything needs users. Not everything needs to be a business. Some of the most valuable things you can build are things only you will ever use.

I have an app that I built for myself that has zero users, zero revenue, and zero chance of becoming a startup. It's one of my favorite things I've ever shipped.

### What it does

It's a simple tool that helps me organize things I need to track across projects I'm working on. Nothing fancy. A few screens, some basic sorting, a way to quickly add entries from my phone.

Could I use Notion for this? Probably. Could I use a spreadsheet? Sure.

But I wanted it to work exactly the way my brain works. Not the way Notion thinks I should organize things. Not the way Google Sheets forces me to format data. My way.

That's the thing about building for yourself — there's no compromise. No feature requests from users who want different things. No product decisions driven by what the market wants. Just a tool that fits your workflow perfectly.

### Why this matters if you're a founder

If you're an entrepreneur or business owner thinking about building an app, your first instinct is probably to build something for customers. Something with a market. Something you can monetize.

I get it. But I'd argue your first app should be for you.

Here's why:

**You learn the process without pressure.** When you're building for yourself, nothing bad happens if it breaks. There's no user complaining, no data lost, no reputation on the line. You get to make mistakes, figure things out, and build confidence in a zero-stakes environment.

**You actually ship something.** The number one reason people don't build is because the first project feels too big and too important. "My app needs to be perfect before I show anyone." If the only user is you, that pressure disappears. You'll build faster and ship sooner.

**You prove to yourself it's possible.** This is the big one. Before you can pitch investors or hire a team or launch a product, you need to know — in your bones — that you can take an idea and turn it into a working thing. Building for yourself gives you that proof.

### The apps nobody talks about

Nobody posts their personal tools on Twitter. Nobody pitches their private automations to VCs. But I guarantee you that the most productive people you know have a handful of apps, scripts, and tools they built just for themselves.

A morning dashboard that shows them exactly what they need to see. A quick-entry tool for logging client calls. A script that formats their invoices. Small things that save 10 minutes a day — which adds up to 60+ hours a year.

### The play

Build something for yourself this week. Not a startup. Not an MVP. Not something to pitch.

Something that makes your day a little easier. Something that saves you a few minutes. Something that works exactly the way you want it to.

Use it for a week. See what's missing. Fix it. That cycle — build, use, fix — is the most valuable skill you can develop. And the fastest way to develop it is by building for an audience of one.
