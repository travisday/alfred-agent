Subject: I built an app in 5 hours at a hackathon

A few months ago I got invited to a hackathon hosted by Cursor. Five hours to build something from scratch. Demo it at the end. That's it.

I want to tell you what happened because I think it changes how you think about what's possible.

### The setup

About 50 people, split into teams. Everyone's using AI coding tools. No pre-built projects, no templates, no head starts. You walk in, form a team, pick an idea, and go.

My team decided to build an AI platform that helps YouTube creators turn their video libraries into searchable, chat-ready assistants. Basically, you upload your YouTube channel and your audience can ask questions and get answers pulled from your actual content.

Is this something we could've hand-coded in 5 hours? Absolutely not. Not even close. There's video processing, transcription, AI embeddings, a chat interface, user auth, and a database to store everything.

### What actually happened

The first hour was planning. We figured out the core feature (search + chat across transcripts), decided what we weren't building (no user accounts, no fancy UI), and split the work.

Then we built. Fast. AI wrote the majority of the code. We directed it, caught mistakes, and stitched things together.

Things broke. Of course they did. The database connections were wrong. The API wasn't returning the right format. The chat interface was pulling irrelevant transcripts. Normal stuff. We fixed it, moved on.

By hour four we had something working. Hour five was cleanup and prep for the demo.

Was it perfect? No. Was it polished? Definitely not. But it worked. You could actually chat with a YouTube creator's content and get real answers. In five hours.

### What this means if you have an idea

I'm not telling you this to brag about hackathons. I'm telling you because of what it proves:

**A working prototype doesn't take months.** It takes a clear idea, the right tools, and a time constraint that forces you to cut everything that doesn't matter.

**The time constraint is the feature.** We built something useful specifically because we only had 5 hours. No time for scope creep. No time for "what if we also added..." No time for perfection. Just the core thing, working.

**A prototype is leverage.** We demoed it. People understood the idea immediately because they could see it, not just hear about it. That's the difference between pitching a slide deck and pitching a product.

### What I'd tell you to do

Give yourself a weekend. Or even just a Saturday.

Pick one idea. Not your best idea — your simplest one. The one you can describe in a sentence.

Set a hard deadline. "By Sunday night, this needs to do one thing and I need to be able to show it to someone."

Then build. Use ChatGPT to plan it. Use a coding tool to build it. Don't worry about polish. Don't worry about edge cases. Get the core thing working and stop.

At the end of the weekend, you'll have something you can demo. You can show it to a co-founder, an investor, a friend, or just yourself. Either way, you'll have proof that you can build. And that proof changes everything.

### One more thing

At the hackathon, I met people who came from all kinds of backgrounds. Not all of them were engineers. Some were founders, some were designers, some were just curious. The AI tools leveled the playing field in a way I hadn't seen before.

If you're thinking "I'm not technical enough for this" — I watched non-technical people ship working apps in an afternoon. The bar is lower than you think.
