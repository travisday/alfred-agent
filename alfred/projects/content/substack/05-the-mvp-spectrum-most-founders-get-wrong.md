Subject: The MVP spectrum most founders get wrong

Everyone says "build an MVP." Nobody tells you there are 5 different levels of MVP and most people are building at the wrong one.

I see it all the time. Someone has an idea for a SaaS product. First thing they do is start thinking about payment processing, user onboarding, analytics dashboards, and a marketing site.

That's level 5. They should be at level 1.

### The 5 levels

**Level 1 — Personal tool**
Built for you. Runs on your machine or a simple deploy. No other users. No login screen. Just something that solves your problem.

This is where everyone should start. Nobody's watching. Nothing can go wrong in a way that matters. You're just testing whether the idea is useful at all.

**Level 2 — Shared tool**
Same thing, but you send the link to 2-3 people you know. Maybe it's a tool for your team. Maybe you share it with a friend who has the same problem. Basic login so each person has their own data. That's it.

**Level 3 — Beta**
Now you're testing with real users — people you don't personally know. You need proper authentication, a real database, some basic error handling. The app doesn't need to be pretty. It needs to work and not lose people's data.

**Level 4 — Product**
Paying customers. Now you need payments, terms of service, support, reliability. The app needs to handle edge cases. Downtime costs you money and trust.

**Level 5 — Scale**
Investor-backed growth. Performance, analytics, infrastructure, team. This is a company, not a project.

### Why this matters

Each level requires different tools, different effort, and a different mindset.

Level 1 doesn't need Stripe. Level 2 doesn't need analytics. Level 3 doesn't need a marketing site.

But most people think "I'm building an app" means level 4 or 5. So they spend weeks setting up infrastructure for users that don't exist yet. They're solving scaling problems before they've confirmed anyone wants the thing.

### Where AI changes the game

AI makes levels 1-3 ridiculously accessible. You can build a personal tool in a day. A shared tool in a weekend. A beta in a couple weeks.

That speed is a superpower — but only if you use it at the right level.

Build at level 1 first. Use it yourself. If it's useful, move to level 2. Share it with people you trust. If they use it, move to level 3. Get real feedback.

Each level earns you the right to go to the next one. Skip levels and you're guessing. Hit them in order and you're building on evidence.

### The leverage play

Here's what most people miss: you don't need to reach level 5 for the idea to be valuable.

A level 1 personal tool that saves you 5 hours a week is already worth it.

A level 3 beta with 50 active users is something you can show an investor, put on a resume, or pitch as a proof of concept.

You don't need a finished product to create leverage. You need proof that the idea works at any level.
